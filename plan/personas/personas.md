# User Personas

Three personas for our browser slot game. Research and sources are in `../raw-research/slot-player-research.md`.

Quick note on scoping: the research splits slot enjoyment into a "light route" (anticipation, theming, social sharing) and a "dark route" (trance-like escape that's linked to problem gambling). We're designing for the light route only. That's why none of these personas are the stereotypical problem-gambler profile.

## Maya, 24 — the casual thrill-seeker

Junior marketing associate. Plays on her phone. Sessions are short, maybe 5 or 10 minutes, usually during a commute or while something else is on in the background.

Maya has never been to a casino. She plays casual mobile games like Candy Crush and Wordle. A friend sent her a browser slot game once and she got hooked on the tap-and-see-what-happens feeling for an afternoon.

What she wants is simple: kill a few minutes with something visually fun, get a small rush when things line up, and not have to read any instructions first.

In the research she's closest to Chen et al.'s Excitement Seekers, which was the biggest cluster in their study. She's getting her enjoyment from the wins themselves, not from zoning out — that's the light route Dixon et al. describe.

What annoys her: signup walls, tutorials, slow load times, cluttered UIs with too many buttons, any hint that the game wants her money.

For us, that means: instant play with no account required, mobile-first layout, one obvious spin button, fast animations, a mute toggle (she might be on the bus).

## Darren, 41 — the themed-experience player

High school history teacher. Plays on his laptop, usually after dinner, for 20 to 40 minutes at a time, two or three nights a week.

Darren has been to Vegas twice. What he liked there wasn't the gambling, it was the atmosphere — the themes, the sounds, the narrative bonus rounds. He plays free online slots occasionally to decompress after grading papers.

He wants a polished themed game world, the kind of slot where you actually care what the symbols mean. Reaching a bonus round is the highlight of the session.

In the research he straddles the Relaxation Seekers and Multipurpose Seekers clusters. He's also a "Slot Enthusiast" in the iGaming industry sense — the segment that responds to bonus features, free spins, and theming. And he's a good example of the perceived-control thing: he likes adjusting his bet even though he knows it doesn't change the odds. The agency feels good regardless.

What annoys him: generic fruit-and-7s themes, confusing paytables, bonus features buried behind long dry spells, no visible session stats.

For us, that means: commit to one visual theme and do it well, make the paytable readable, build at least one special feature (bonus round or free spins) that players can work toward, let him adjust his bet, show session stats somewhere.

## Priya, 19 — the social sharer

CS sophomore. Very high tech comfort. Plays in short bursts, usually because a friend sent her a link, and sends things back around if there's something worth sharing.

Her interest in a slot game is half ironic and half genuine. She'll play a weird browser game for ten minutes because someone dropped it in the group chat. Whether she sends it back to anyone depends entirely on whether the game gives her something worth sharing: a funny theme, a screenshot-able moment, a score to brag about.

She doesn't show up cleanly in Chen et al.'s framework because that study was about real-money casino players. In Bartle's taxonomy she's a Socializer (playing for the social layer, not the mechanics), and in iGaming industry segmentation she'd be a Game Hopper (tries lots of things briefly). The dopamine hit for her isn't just the win, it's showing someone the win.

What annoys her: games with nothing to share, signup walls that break the link-in-the-group-chat flow, anything that looks like it wants her credit card, games that take themselves too seriously.

For us, that means: win screens that look good as screenshots, visual personality with an actual point of view, no signup wall, and maybe a simple leaderboard or "my best today" if we want a light competitive hook.

## Quick comparison

| Need                     | Maya         | Darren       | Priya        |
| ------------------------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------------ |
| No signup required       | critical     | preferred    | critical     |
| Mobile responsive        | critical     | nice-to-have | critical     |
| Adjustable bet size      | optional     | important    | optional     |
| Bonus round / features   | nice-to-have | critical     | nice-to-have |
| Strong visual theme      | important    | critical     | important    |
| Shareable result screens | nice-to-have | nice-to-have | critical     |
| Session stats display    | optional     | important    | optional     |
| Mute / audio controls    | critical     | important    | important    |

Things all three need: loads fast, works in a browser without an install, one clear primary action, respects the mute toggle, wins that feel like wins.

Where they split: session length (Maya wants 3 minutes, Darren wants 30). Depth (Darren wants it, the other two don't). Social (Priya needs it, Maya and Darren don't care).
